Family of Teaching Models (Bruce Joyce & Marsha Weil)

Teaching models are structured plans or patterns that can be used to shape a curriculum, design instructional materials, and guide instruction in the classroom. They are grouped into four major families:

Family of Models Model of Teaching Developer(s) Core Objective / Focus

(Focuses on how learners process, organize, and retrieve information)		 Advance Organizer Model David Ausubel Strengthens cognitive structures by providing a general overview of a subject before introducing specific details.

Inductive Thinking Model Hilda Taba Trains students to form concepts and rules by moving from specific facts to generalizations.
Inquiry Training Model Richard Suchman Helps students ask questions, form hypotheses, and logically analyze puzzling phenomena.
Cognitive Growth Model Jean Piaget Directs instructional strategies to align with the learner’s current stage of cognitive development.

(Focuses on collective problem-solving, relationships, and democracy)		 Jurisprudential Inquiry Donald Oliver & James Shaver Guides students to systematically analyze legal, ethical, and controversial public policy issues.

Cooperative Learning / Positive Interdependence David Johnson, Roger Johnson & Robert Slavin Structuring group work so that individual and group successes are completely linked.

(Focuses on individual psychological health, self-actualization, and emotional growth)		 Non-Directive Teaching Carl Rogers Focuses on student-centered growth; the teacher acts as a facilitator helping students set and evaluate their own goals.

Simulation Carl Smith Employs life-like scenarios in a controlled environment to practice decision-making.

Anxiety Reduction / Assertiveness Training Joseph Wolpe / Andrew Salter Pairs deep relaxation with progressive steps to overcome anxiety or teach honest interpersonal skills.

Syntax of the Group Investigation Model (Dewey & Thelen)

The Group Investigation Model follows a structured democratic process where students actively solve a problem together. The logical progression of its phases is as follows:

Phase Step Name Core Activities
Phase 1 C - Students encounter puzzling situation The teacher presents a provocative, challenging, or puzzling situation to spark curiosity and interest.
Phase 2 E - Students explore reactions to the situation Students express their initial reactions, share thoughts, and begin to brainstorm different dimensions of the problem.
Phase 3 A - Students formulate study task and organize for study The group defines the specific problem to be researched, formulates questions, and organizes themselves into sub-groups based on tasks or roles.
Phase 4 D - Independent and Group study Students carry out data gathering, research, and analysis both individually and collaboratively.
Phase 5 B - Students analyze progress and process Students evaluate their own findings, review the group's progress, and reflect on their problem-solving process (often followed by a presentation or integration phase).

TOPIC 10: MAXIMS OF TEACHING

  • Simple to complex: Start with simple ideas, then move to complex structures.
  • Known to unknown: Link new knowledge with what the student already knows.
  • Seen to unseen: Present physical objects first, then past/future abstract concepts.
  • Concrete to abstract: Teach using tactile objects before introducing logic.
  • Particular to general: Show examples first, then derive formulas/laws (Inductive method).
  • Whole to part: Provide an overview first, then explain details.
  • Analysis to synthesis: Break a concept down into components, then connect them together.
  • Training of senses: Utilize sight, hearing, and touch to reinforce learning.

TOPIC 13: BLOOM'S TAXONOMY (HIGH PRIORITY)

In teaching aptitude, educational objectives are categorized into three distinct domains, often referred to as the KSA Bio-triangle (Knowledge, Skills, and Attitudes). This classification ensures a holistic approach to teaching and assessment.

1. Cognitive Domain (The "Head" - Thinking/Knowledge)

Developed by Benjamin Bloom (1956) and revised by Lorin Anderson & David Krathwohl (2001). It focuses on intellectual skills, knowledge acquisition, and critical thinking. They are structured from lowest complexity to highest complexity:

Classic Level (1956 - Noun) Revised Level (2001 - Verb) Core Meaning / Description Key Action Verbs
1. Knowledge Remembering Recalling factual data, terms, and basic concepts. Define, List, State, Identify, Name, Label
2. Comprehension Understanding Grasping the meaning of informational material; translating or interpreting. Paraphrase, Classify, Summarize, Convert, Describe
3. Application Applying Using abstract concepts or rules in concrete, real-world situations. Solve, Calculate, Demonstrate, Use, Modify, Implement
4. Analysis Analyzing Breaking down information into parts to see how they relate to each other. Differentiate, Separate, Outline, Sift, Compare, Contrast
5. Synthesis Evaluating Making judgments about the value of ideas, methods, or materials based on criteria. (Moved down in 2001) Appraise, Criticize, Defend, Justify, Judge, Rate
6. Evaluation Creating Putting parts together to form a unique, original whole. (Moved to top spot in 2001) Formulate, Design, Create, Compose, Plan, Construct

2. Affective Domain (The "Heart" - Feelings/Attitudes)

Developed by David Krathwohl (1964). It deals with emotional growth, values, motivations, attitudes, and how learners handle things emotionally. Hierarchical levels from lowest to highest:

Level Hierarchy & Description Key Action Verbs / Keywords
1. Receiving (Awareness) Willingness to listen or pay attention to a stimulus (e.g., listening politely to a lecture). Listen, attend, notice, look, share attention
2. Responding Active participation; the student reacts to the stimulus, not just observes it. Discuss, practice, volunteer, reply, answer, assist
3. Valuing Attaching a specific worth or value to an object, phenomenon, or behavior. Argue, respect, support, cherish, complete, invite
4. Organization Prioritizing and resolving conflicts between different values to build an internally consistent value system. Systematize, arrange, integrate, formulate, organize, defend
5. Characterization Highest level; internalizing values so thoroughly that they completely control behavior and become a lifestyle or philosophy of life. Internalize, practice regularly, display, exemplify, influence

3. Psychomotor Domain (The "Hand" - Physical Skills)

Primarily adapted in UGC NET from R.H. Dave’s hierarchy (1970). It covers physical movement, coordination, and motor-skill areas. Hierarchical levels from lowest to highest:

Level Hierarchy & Description Key Action Verbs / Keywords
1. Imitation Observing a skill and attempting to copy it or repeat it mechanically. Copy, mimic, follow, repeat, replicate
2. Manipulation Performing an action from memory or following written/verbal instructions rather than just copying a demonstration. Execute, perform, build, operate, construct (from directions)
3. Precision Refining a physical skill to make it exact, accurate, and stable without needing instructions. Master, perfect, accurately perform, control, calibrate
4. Articulation Coordinating a series of physical movements or skills sequentially and consistently. Combine, integrate, coordinate, adapt, solve complex tasks
5. Naturalization Highest level; automating the skill completely. The physical task is performed effortlessly, naturally, and with extreme confidence (unconscious competence). Automate, naturally design, fluidly operate, orchestrate

💡 UGC NET High-Yield Exam Tips

• The "Peaks": Always remember the highest levels because they are common target areas for multiple-choice questions: Creating (Cognitive), Characterization (Affective), and Naturalization (Psychomotor).

• Action Verb Traps: Pay attention to verbs like Paraphrase or Summarize—they belong to Comprehension / Understanding, not Knowledge / Remembering.

Teacher-Centered vs. Learner-Centered Methods

  • Teacher-Centered Methods (Traditional):
    • Lecture Method: Most common "one-way" communication. Large amounts of content delivered quickly, but student participation is low.
    • Demonstration Method: Teacher "shows" how something works. Better than pure lecture as it involves visual learning.
    • Team Teaching: Multiple teachers plan and teach the same group of students.
  • Learner-Centered Methods (Modern):
    • Heuristic Method (Dr. H.E. Armstrong): Students act as independent discoverers. The teacher provides the problem, and the student finds the solution.
    • Project Method (William Kilpatrick): Learning by doing. Students work on a purposeful activity in a social environment.
    • Assignment & Discussion: Encourages self-study and critical thinking.

The Six Thinking Hats System (Edward de Bono, 1985)

The Six Thinking Hats is a powerful tool for group discussion and individual thinking. It replaces traditional adversarial arguing (where people clash or debate) with Parallel Thinking, where everyone in a group looks in the same direction and focuses on the same perspective at the same time.

Objective: To separate emotion from logic, creativity from information, and to prevent cognitive overload by tackling one perspective at a time.

Hat Color Core Focus / Type Mechanism & Description Exam Keywords
🤍 White Hat The Data Hat Purely objective and neutral. It deals with what information is currently available, what is missing, and what data needs to be gathered. Facts, figures, data gathering, information gaps, neutral
❤️ Red Hat The Emotion Hat Allows learners or team members to express fears, likes, dislikes, and hunches immediately without any need for logical justification. Intuition, feelings, hunches, emotions, no justification
🖤 Black Hat The Caution Hat Logical negative perspective. Identifies why a proposal might fail, design flaws, regulations, and potential dangers. Caution, risk, judgment, logical negative, why it won't work
💛 Yellow Hat The Optimism Hat Logical positive perspective. Deliberately looks for the upside, advantages, feasible outcomes, and why an idea will succeed. Optimism, benefits, values, advantages, logical positive
💚 Green Hat The Creativity Hat Focuses on generating out-of-the-box ideas, alternatives, modifications, and creative provocations to solve difficulties. Creativity, alternatives, proposals, provocations, new possibilities
💙 Blue Hat The Process Hat The orchestrator hat (worn by facilitator/teacher). Manages the thinking process, ensures guidelines are followed, switches hats, and guides to final summaries. Control, process management, agenda, summaries, overview

💡 Exam Flashpoints

• Parallel Thinking: If a question asks about a framework designed to facilitate "parallel thinking" or reduce ego-driven arguments in classroom debates, the answer is always Six Thinking Hats.

• Distractors: Exams often insert fake colors like a Brown Hat or Violet Hat. Remember only the primary six: White, Red, Black, Yellow, Green, and Blue.

• Logical Negative vs. Positive: Black Hat = Logical Negative (reality check); Yellow Hat = Logical Positive (finding benefits).

Mind-Mapping and Key Graphical Tools

Mind-Mapping (Tony Buzan): A non-linear, graphical technique that mimics the brain's natural, radiant way of thinking. It uses a mix of images, colors, lines, and words to map out information radiating outward from a central idea. It relies heavily on evocative images rather than dense text to trigger associations.

Comparison of Graphical & Problem-Solving Tools

Tool / Technique Primary Function Core Mechanism
Mind-Mapping Knowledge organization & creative association. Uses visual hierarchy, colors, and evocative images radiating from a central idea.
Concept Mapping Showing structural relationships between distinct ideas. Uses nodes (concepts) connected by labeled arrows/linking words (e.g., "leads to", "is a part of").
Brainstorming Rapid idea generation. Focuses on quantity over quality in a judgment-free zone; usually textual or verbal.
Storyboarding Linear sequencing & narrative planning. A series of illustrations or images displayed in sequence to visualize a timeline or animation.
Synectics Creative problem-solving. Relies on the use of analogies and metaphors ("making the strange familiar").

💡 Exam Flashpoints

• Mind-Mapping vs Concept Mapping: If the question emphasizes images being more evocative than words and triggering wide associations, the answer is always Mind-Mapping. If it emphasizes relationships between cross-linked concepts with connecting lines, it is Concept Mapping.

• MOODLE: If a question points to an open-source platform or dynamic learning environments that integrate these tools, watch out for MOODLE (Modular Object-Oriented Dynamic Learning Environment).

TOPIC 18: LEARNER'S CHARACTERISTICS

Learning Definition

"Learning is a relatively permanent change in behaviour or capacity acquired through experience."

Individual Differences - Field Dependency

Field Independent Learners Field Dependent Learners

Thinking Styles

  • Reflective: Relates new information to past experiences, asks "why?".
  • Creative: Creates unique solutions, takes cognitive shortcuts.
  • Practical: Focuses on concrete facts, simplest way to complete tasks.
  • Conceptual: Demands the big picture, wants to know how components interact.

Parenting Styles Matrix (Diana Baumrind)

Parenting styles differ along two key dimensions: Warmth/Responsiveness (acceptance and support) and Control/Demandingness (rules and supervision). This results in four parenting styles:

Parenting Style Warmth / Responsiveness Control / Demandingness Description & Characteristics
a) Authoritative High High Set clear rules and expectations but are highly supportive, warm, and open to communication. (Considered the most balanced style).
b) Authoritarian Low High Highly controlling, strict, and expect blind obedience ("Because I said so") with little emotional warmth or discussion.
c) Permissive High Low Very warm, loving, and nurturing but set few boundaries, rarely enforce rules, and avoid confrontation.
d) Rejecting / Neglecting Low Low Uninvolved, emotionally detached, and provide little to no guidance, rules, or emotional support.

Core Intelligence and Developmental Theories

1. Theory of Fluid & Crystallized Intelligence (Cattell & Horn)

General intelligence is split into two distinct, interactive components:

  • Fluid Intelligence ($G_f$ - Raymond Cattell): The native, inherited capacity to think logically, reason abstractly, and solve novel problems independent of any acquired knowledge or schooling (e.g., pattern recognition, memory span).
    Exam Flashpoint: Peaks in late adolescence/young adulthood and gradually declines with age due to biological degeneration.
  • Crystallized Intelligence ($G_c$ - John Horn): The ability to use accumulated knowledge, skills, vocabulary, and experience deeply rooted in education and cultural familiarity.
    Exam Flashpoint: Continues to grow or remain stable throughout adulthood and late old age.

2. Lawrence Kohlberg's Theory of Moral Development

Kohlberg proposed that moral reasoning progresses through 3 levels and 6 stages, driven by cognitive growth and social experiences:

Level Focus / Drivers Stages
1. Pre-conventional Driven purely by external consequences (rules are external rather than internalized). Stage 1: Obedience and Punishment Orientation
Stage 2: Individualism and Exchange (Self-interest)
2. Conventional Driven by societal expectations, laws, social order, and approval from others. Stage 3: Good Interpersonal Relationships (Approval)
Stage 4: Maintaining the Social Order (Law & Order)
3. Post-conventional Driven by internal ethical frameworks, universal human rights, and social contracts. Stage 5: Social Contract and Individual Rights
Stage 6: Universal Ethical Principles

3. Identity Status Theory & Identity Moratorium (James Marcia)

Expanding on Erik Erikson's adolescence stage (Identity vs. Role Confusion), James Marcia classified adolescents into four identity statuses based on two criteria: Exploration (crisis) and Commitment:

Identity Status Exploration (Crisis) Commitment Core Characteristics
Identity Achievement High High Has explored options and made a firm commitment (found themselves).
Identity Moratorium High Low Active exploration of roles and values without final commitment (actively searching).
Identity Foreclosure Low High Adopts parents' or authority's choices blindly without personal exploration.
Identity Diffusion Low Low No direction, no active exploration, and no commitment.

Erik Erikson’s Eight Stages of Psychosocial Development

Erikson's theory defines eight developmental stages through which a healthy developing human should pass from infancy to late adulthood. Each stage is characterized by a psychosocial crisis that yields a basic virtue.

Stage Psychosocial Crisis Approximate Age Basic Virtue Core Focus / Major Event
1 Trust vs. Mistrust Infancy (0 to 18 months) Hope Feeding & comfort; reliability of caregivers meeting basic needs.
2 Autonomy vs. Shame & Doubt Toddlerhood (18 months to 3 years) Will Toilet training & independence; asserting control over clothing/food choices.

Detailed Breakdown of the 8 Stages

  • 1. Trust vs. Mistrust: Center is reliability of caregivers. Success brings hope; failure leads to lifelong fear and suspicion.
  • 2. Autonomy vs. Shame and Doubt: Children assertion of independence. Encouragement develops will/confidence; punishment or over-control leads to self-doubt.
  • 3. Initiative vs. Guilt: Exerting power and control over play. Success brings purpose; disapproval or overly rigid control creates guilt.
  • 4. Industry vs. Inferiority: Learning formal skills (academic success). Pride and competence follow success; negative comparison leads to feelings of inadequacy.
  • 5. Identity vs. Role Confusion: Adolescents establishing individual values. Success leads to fidelity (staying true to self); failure leads to identity crisis and role confusion.
  • 6. Intimacy vs. Isolation: Forming long-term committed love relationships. Avoiding intimacy can lead to loneliness, isolation, and depression.
  • 7. Generativity vs. Stagnation: Career, community, and parenting contribution. Success results in care; failure leads to stagnation and feeling stuck.
  • 8. Ego Integrity vs. Despair: Reflection in old age. A sense of fulfillment brings wisdom; looking back with regret brings bitterness and despair.

Gary Borich's Framework of Effective Teaching Behaviors

Educational psychologist Gary Borich identified 10 classroom behaviors that correlate directly with positive student outcomes and higher test scores. He divided these into 5 Key Behaviors (essential) and 5 Helping Behaviors (supportive).

The 5 Key Behaviors (Mnemonic: L-I-T-E-S)

Key Behavior Description / Technical Characteristics Classroom Implementation
1. Lesson Clarity Presenting concepts logically, clearly, and step-by-step so that ideas are easy to comprehend. Speaking audibly, minimizing distracting jargon, organizing the board well, and explaining concepts directly.
2. Instructional Variety The flexibility, diversity, and variability shown by the teacher when presenting a lesson to sustain interest. Shifting pacing, changing voice modulation/tone, using mixed media, and moving around the classroom.
3. Teacher Task Orientation The amount of classroom time dedicated purely to teaching and academic content rather than administration. Minimizing disruptions, keeping attendance taking brief, handling misbehavior quickly, and maximizing subject-matter time.
4. Engagement in Learning Actively involving students in the lesson so that they are mentally participating, not just sitting passively. Constant monitoring of seatwork/group activities, setting a fixed schedule, and high-interest exercises.
5. Student Success Rate Designing instruction so that students spend a high percentage of time on tasks with moderate-to-high success rate. Ensuring new material builds logically upon prior knowledge to prevent overwhelming frustration.

The 5 Helping Behaviors

  • Using Student Ideas and Contributions: Acknowledging, rephrasing, modifying, or applying student answers to drive the direction of the lecture.
  • Structuring Lesson Content: Providing advanced frameworks, organizers, or summaries at the beginning or end of a lesson to firmly tie old knowledge to new information.
  • Questioning: Asking both content questions (lower-order, factual recall) and process questions (higher-order, requiring generalizations, inferences, and deep thinking).
  • Probing: Prompting and encouraging students to expand, clarify, or elaborate further on their initial response, rather than moving on immediately.
  • Teacher Affect: Fostering a warm, supportive environment by displaying enthusiasm, excitement, healthy eye contact, and authentic passion for the subject.

Maximum Performance vs. Typical Response Tests

Psychological and educational assessments are broadly categorized into maximum performance (measuring limits of ability) and typical response (measuring common behavior/personality traits).

1. Maximum Performance Tests

These tests assess the upper limits of an examinee's knowledge, cognitive capacity, and abilities under best effort. They have right/wrong answers:

  • Achievement Tests: Measure past learning and acquired knowledge to determine how much a student has learned after specific instruction (e.g., classroom quizzes, board exams). Key Question: "What has the individual already learned?"
  • Aptitude Tests: Measure future potential and innate capacity to learn, predicting how well an individual is likely to perform or adapt in a new environment/training (e.g., GRE, GMAT, Teaching Aptitude tests). Key Question: "What is the individual capable of learning in the future?"

2. Typical Response Tests (Personality Assessment)

These tests evaluate an examinee's typical behavior, interests, attitudes, and psychological traits. There are no right or wrong answers:

  • Objective Personality Tests: Highly structured, clear, and unambiguous stimuli (e.g., rating scales, True/False statements like MMPI, Big Five Index). Scoring is standardized and free from examiner bias.
  • Projective Personality Tests: Unstructured, vague, and ambiguous stimuli (e.g., open-ended responses to inkblots or pictures like Rorschach Inkblot, Thematic Apperception Test [TAT]). Examinees project their unconscious motives/conflicts onto the ambiguous material. Scoring is qualitative and relies heavily on interpretation.

Comparison Summary

Test Category Primary Objective Key Examples
Achievement Evaluates specific, previously learned knowledge. Classroom quizzes, Board exams.
Aptitude Predicts capability to acquire new skills. IQ tests, Career aptitude tests, GRE.
Objective Personality Profiles explicit traits via structured self-reporting. MMPI, Myers-Briggs (MBTI), Big Five.
Projective Personality Uncovers hidden or unconscious psychological patterns. Rorschach Inkblot Test, Thematic Apperception Test (TAT).

TOPIC 25: CHOICE BASED CREDIT SYSTEM (CBCS)

Key Course Components

  • Core Course: Compulsory course that all students must complete.
  • Elective Course: Chosen from a pool of courses; can be discipline-specific or generic.
  • Ability Enhancement Courses (AEC): Focuses on skill enhancement or environmental education.

Advantages

  • Shifts focus from teacher-centric to student-centric education.
  • Allows interdisciplinary credit transfers across institutions.
  • Enables students to choose a pace that matches their learning capacity.
